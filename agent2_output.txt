## 4. Quick Wins (Results Within 2 Weeks)

These are high-impact, low-effort actions that will produce visible results before the end of Week 2. They are designed to build momentum, demonstrate commitment, and create early evidence of growth.

### Quick Win 1: Rewrite & Share One RFC (Week 1)
**Resource:** LR009 — Writing Effective RFCs and Design Documents
**Effort:** 4-hour workshop + 1-2 hours writing
**Action:** Attend the RFC workshop. Within 48 hours, rewrite your most impactful recent technical decision as a well-structured RFC. Share it with 2+ engineers outside Platform Engineering and your skip-level manager.
**Why It's a Quick Win:** A single well-written RFC shared cross-team immediately changes how others perceive your communication skills. It creates a durable artifact that travels through the organization without you being in the room. This directly attacks the 2.8 cross-team communication score.
**Visible Result:** Cross-team engineers see and engage with your written thinking for the first time. Your skip-level manager has a concrete artifact demonstrating Staff-level communication.

### Quick Win 2: Transform Your 1:1s (Week 2)
**Resource:** LR005 — Mentoring Skills for Technical Leaders
**Effort:** 4-hour course + 30 minutes prep
**Action:** Complete the LinkedIn Learning course. Before your next 1:1 with your direct report, restructure the agenda using the career conversation framework. Open with: "I want to make our 1:1s more valuable for your growth. What do you want to be doing in 12 months?"
**Why It's a Quick Win:** Your direct report has explicitly asked for better 1:1s and career conversations. Responding to specific feedback with visible behavior change is the fastest way to improve a relationship and build your reputation as a developer of people. Staff Engineers are evaluated on whether they grow others.
**Visible Result:** Direct report experiences an immediately better 1:1. Word travels. Your manager hears you're investing in people.

### Quick Win 3: Show Up at the Leadership Circle (Week 1)
**Resource:** LR008 — Women in Tech Leadership Circle
**Effort:** 2 hours (attend the next scheduled meetup)
**Action:** Attend the next meeting. Introduce yourself to at least 3 people. Share that you're working toward Staff Engineer and are investing in your development. Ask one senior attendee: "What's one thing you wish you'd done differently on your path to senior leadership?"
**Why It's a Quick Win:** Showing up is the hardest part. One meeting expands your network beyond Platform Engineering, gives you potential sponsors, and makes you visible to the ERG leadership (who often sit on promotion and talent review committees).
**Visible Result:** 3+ new cross-functional relationships. A potential mentor or sponsor connection. A sense of community that sustains the harder work ahead.

### Quick Win 4: Sign Up to Give an Architecture Talk (Week 2)
**Resource:** LR006 — System Design Architecture Talks
**Effort:** 30 minutes to sign up + 1 hour to attend one as audience
**Action:** Attend one Architecture Talk session as an audience member in Week 2. Before you leave, sign up for a presentation slot in Week 5. Choose a topic you already know well (a recent platform decision, a system design you led). The act of committing creates accountability.
**Why It's a Quick Win:** The commitment itself is the win. Signing up for a public presentation signals to yourself and others that you are stepping into visibility. The actual talk is in Week 5, giving you 3 weeks to prepare.
**Visible Result:** Your name appears on the Architecture Talks schedule. Colleagues notice. You've made a public commitment that's hard to back out of.

### Quick Win 5: Start a Brag Document (Week 1)
**Resource:** No formal resource — personal practice
**Effort:** 30 minutes to set up + 5 minutes/day ongoing
**Action:** Create a running document (Google Doc, Notion page, or markdown file) titled "Priya's Impact Log." Every day, spend 5 minutes writing down: (1) what you accomplished, (2) who it impacted, (3) what you learned. This document becomes the raw material for your promotion packet, performance reviews, and self-advocacy conversations.
**Why It's a Quick Win:** Priya's assertiveness gap includes "doesn't take credit for work." A brag document externalizes the habit of tracking impact. It's also the single most recommended practice from every Staff Engineer who's been through the promotion process. Starting it in Week 1 means 13 weeks of documented impact by the pathway's end.
**Visible Result:** By Week 13, you have a comprehensive record of your contributions that makes writing your promotion packet straightforward rather than agonizing.

## 5. Deep Investments (Sustained Growth Programs)

These are the larger commitments that drive transformational (not incremental) growth. They require significant time, and in some cases budget, but they deliver the deepest, most durable skill development.

### Deep Investment 1: Crucial Conversations Workshop (LR003)
**Investment:** 16 hours + $500 | Weeks 9-10
**Why It's Worth It:** Assertiveness is the development area most likely to be dismissed as "personality" rather than skill. It is not. It is a learnable, practicable behavior — but it requires immersive, scenario-based training with real-time feedback. A self-paced course will not build this muscle. The 16-hour workshop format with extensive role-play is specifically designed to create the psychological safety and repeated practice needed to shift deeply ingrained communication patterns. ## 6. Community & Networking Opportunities

> **Strategic Context:** Priya's collaboration gradient (3.8-4.0 same-team vs. 3.0 cross-team) reveals that her professional network is concentrated within Platform Engineering. For Staff Engineer promotion, she needs to be known and respected across the engineering organization. These community and networking opportunities are not "nice-to-haves" — they are the connective tissue that makes all other development areas stick.

### 6.1 Women in Tech Leadership Circle (LR008)
- **Cadence:** Monthly, 2 hours/session
- **Starting:** Week 1 (immediate)
- **Networking Strategy:**
  - Month 1: Attend. Introduce yourself to 3 people. Learn who the senior women leaders are.
  - Month 2: Share a challenge. Ask for advice. Identify one person who could be a sponsor.
  - Month 3: Volunteer for a lightning talk. Offer to help organize an event.
  - Month 4+: Become a regular. Mentor a newer attendee. Build reciprocal relationships.
- **Target Connections:** Women at Staff+ level from Product, Infrastructure, and Data Engineering. These are the cross-functional relationships that accelerate Priya's visibility and provide sponsorship during promotion discussions.

### 6.2 Architecture Talks Community (LR006)
- **Cadence:** Recurring (biweekly or monthly, varies by program)
- **Starting:** Week 2 (attend), Week 5 (present)
- **Networking Strategy:**
  - Attending talks by senior engineers builds awareness of how Staff-level engineers communicate.
  - Presenting your own talks positions you as a technical authority and makes you visible to the senior engineers who attend.
  - After each talk (yours or others'), follow up with the presenter or an audience member via Slack or email: "I found your point about X really interesting. I've been thinking about a similar problem in Platform — could we chat for 15 minutes?"
- **Target Connections:** Principal Engineers, Staff Engineers, and Engineering Managers who attend Architecture Talks. These are the people who will vouch for Priya's technical judgment in promotion calibrations.

### 6.3 Cross-Team Coffee Chats (Self-Directed Practice)
- **Cadence:** 1 per week, 30 minutes each
- **Starting:** Week 5 (after Influence Without Authority Modules 1-3)
- **Networking Strategy:**
  - Use the stakeholder map from LR007 to identify engineers, PMs, and TPMs on teams that depend on or interact with Platform Engineering.
  - Schedule 30-minute informal coffee chats (virtual or in-person).
  - Use this framework for each conversation:
    1. "What are the biggest challenges your team is facing right now?"
    2. "How does Platform Engineering's work affect your team?"
    3. "Is there anything we could do differently that would make your team's life easier?"
  - Document insights and share relevant ones with your manager and team.
- **Target Connections:** 8-10 cross-team engineers and PMs over the 13-week pathway. Prioritize teams where collaboration friction has been highest.
- **Why This Matters:** These conversations do three things at once: (1) build the relationships that raise Priya's cross-team score, (2) surface insights that inform Priya's technical strategy work, and (3) make Priya known as someone who proactively seeks to understand the broader system — a Staff Engineer behavior.

### 6.4 Peer Learning Partnership (Self-Directed)
- **Cadence:** Biweekly, 45 minutes each
- **Starting:** Week 3
- **How to Set Up:** Identify one peer (IC4 or aspiring IC5) who is also working on their development. Commit to biweekly check-ins where you each share:
  1. What skill you practiced this week
  2. What went well
  3. Where you struggled
  4. What you'll try next
- **Why This Matters:** Behavior change is hard to sustain alone. A peer accountability partner provides honest feedback, emotional support, and the social commitment that prevents backsliding. Ideally, find someone from a different team to simultaneously build a cross-functional relationship.

### 6.5 Skip-Level & Sponsor Cultivation
- **Cadence:** Quarterly (or when opportunity arises)
- **Starting:** Week 6 (after initial communication skill-building)
- **Strategy:**
  - Identify 1-2 senior leaders (Director+ level) who have visibility into promotion decisions for your organization.
  - Request a 30-minute skip-level conversation. Frame it as: "I'm working toward Staff Engineer and would value your perspective on what you look for in candidates."
  - Share your RFC or technical strategy document as evidence of your work.
  - After the conversation, follow up with a brief thank-you and periodic updates on your progress.
- **Why This Matters:** Sponsors — not mentors — drive promotions. Sponsors are senior leaders who advocate for you in rooms you're not in. Priya needs at least one sponsor who can speak to her impact and readiness during calibration discussions. This does not happen by accident; it must be cultivated intentionally.
